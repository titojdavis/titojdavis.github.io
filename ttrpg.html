Table Top RPG Design

As a longtime Tabletop RPG gamer, I've started to understand exactly what I'm looking for in my games mechanics-wise. Why did D&D 5e feel so shallow? What about D&D 3.5 (the system I grew up reading cover to cover even when I had nobody to play with) was difficult for new players, yet captured my childhood imagination so?

I decided to design my own system that incorporated the character building freedom of 3.5 and Pathfinder 2e with the simplicity of combat layout of Index Card RPG by Runehammer, with mechanics from the Fantasy Flight Star Wars RPG thrown in to balance things out.

Zones

The Core Mechanic that separates the game from traditional D&D is how it handles combat. The grid system is a carry over from D&D's wargaming roots, and it was one of the parts of the game that really slows down play, especially with new players.

A zone would represent a space where everyone occupying a zone is within melee range of each other. A player on their turn would have 3 actions; two can be for anything, one can be for anything that isn't combat. Moving from one zone to another with no difficult terrain complications would take one action.

For example: "You approach a castle and find a pair of guards standing in front of a bridge spanning the castle moat. There is also a pair of guards on the castle battlements with bows."

The zones are, the grass before the bridge, the bridge, the grass at the castle gate after the bridge, and the battlements. To move to the battlements, the GM would call for a climb check that may take two actions rather than one, or even all three for a player's whole turn, but it simplifies combat immensely. Four zones you can occupy, maybe only two can go abreast on the bridge, and a climb check to move to the battlements, and the GM has set the scene. No grid or counting squares necessary.

Character Building

The biggest difference I found between 3.5e and 5e is how characters are built. 3.5 allowed for so much more freedom and building as you go, while 5e more sets the player on a path from the start.

Pathfinder 2e did character building wonderfully. Each class gets a choice of class feats every other level, as well as additional choice within race selection. My game adopts this style, giving a catalogue of feats for each class to build as they go.

Allowing a player to select class feats every other level also lead to nerfing the power of each feat in question, which ends up helping the problem of feeling pressured into sticking exactly to one particular mechanic to maximize character power. With a diversity of weaker individual feats, experimentation and choosing for flavor is encouraged.

Layout

In my opinion, just as important as the game mechanics themselves are how the rules are taught. I started to learn Figma in an attempt to make the pages teaching the rules as well laid out and digestible as possible.

I thought of the games and pages I liked in the tabletop rpg space, and which I didn't, and realized that I needed to keep walls of text small, and any elements that could be represented in a list or chart should be.
